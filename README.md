# Meridian Financial Capstone — Capstone Programme

**5 Tasks · 10 Weeks · Real BA Portfolio**

---

## What This Is

A capstone BA programme built for a mentorship context. The mentee works as a Junior BA on a real regulatory compliance problem at a fictional UK fintech — Meridian Financial — and produces a professional portfolio of BA deliverables across the full project lifecycle.

AI is embedded as the working environment throughout, not added as an afterthought.

---

## Programme Overview

| Task | Name | Weeks | Core Output |
|------|------|-------|-------------|
| **Task 1** | Discovery & AS-IS Analysis | 1–2 | Stakeholder register, AS-IS process map, gap analysis, discovery report |
| **Task 2** | Requirements Engineering | 3–4 | Requirements package (FR + NFR), prioritised backlog, review notes |
| **Task 3** | Solution Design & Integration | 5–6 | Requirements-to-systems mapping, integration gaps, solution options, data model gaps |
| **Task 4** | UAT Planning & Test Scenarios | 7–8 | UAT strategy, test scenario register, traceability matrix |
| **Task 5** | Executive Presentation & Regulatory Submission | 9–10 | Executive summary, 10-slide deck, Q&A prep, FCA submission draft |

---

## How Each Task Works

1. **Mentee receives a brief** — a memo or email from a Meridian stakeholder
2. **Mentee conducts BA work** — using the prompt library as their working environment
3. **Mentee reviews AI output** — using the critique checklists; refines and overrides
4. **Mentee submits deliverables** — all formatted as professional BA outputs
5. **Mentor reviews** — feedback focuses on BA judgement, not whether the answer was "right"

---

## AI Integration

Every task includes:
- **Prompt templates** for AI-assisted research, drafting, and challenge
- **Critique checklists** for reviewing AI output before submission
- **Red lines** documenting where AI consistently fails in BA contexts
- **Verification requirements** — mentee must verify AI claims (especially regulatory citations)

The mentor's role in reviewing AI output is as important as reviewing BA thinking. Hallucinated FCA citations and generic acceptance criteria are the most common failures.

## Key Design Decisions

**Why Meridian Financial?**
FCA-regulated fintech = real regulatory stakes, real compliance language, real professional context. The 90-day deadline is real. The gap between what the FCA wants and what 3 engineers can build in 10 weeks is a genuine tension the mentee has to navigate.

**Why AI is embedded in every task, not just one?**
The BA role increasingly involves working alongside AI tools. This programme teaches the discipline of knowing when to trust AI output and when to override it — which is the actual skill employers are now hiring for.

**Why the prompt library?**
Most BA professionals are given no guidance on how to use AI tools effectively. The prompt library gives mentees proven starting points, while the critique checklists force the higher-order thinking that makes the BA irreplaceable.

**Why Tasks 3 and 4 are in the middle?**
Most BA programmes teach requirements but skip solution design and testing. In reality, the BA owns the problem end-to-end — they must be able to assess feasibility, spot integration gaps, and write test scenarios that validate their requirements. Skipping these steps produces BAs who write requirements without understanding consequences.

## Mentor Notes

**Key review focus areas per task:**
- Task 1: Is the gap analysis evidence-based? Are regulatory citations real?
- Task 2: Are acceptance criteria actually testable? Is the prioritisation logical?
- Task 3: Are vendor recommendations verifiable? Is the solution approach realistic?
- Task 4: Are failure paths plausible? Is coverage complete?
- Task 5: Is the narrative honest? Can the mentee defend every claim?

**AI output is a reviewable artefact.** The mentor should ask the mentee: "Show me what AI gave you and what you changed and why." This is as important as reviewing the final deliverable.

**There is no single right answer.** The Meridian scenario has genuine ambiguity — vendor selection involves trade-offs, gap severity involves judgement, timeline decisions involve risk appetite. The mentor should evaluate the quality of reasoning, not whether the mentee chose the "correct" option.
